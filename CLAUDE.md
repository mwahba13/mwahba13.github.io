# mwahba13.github.io — Project Instructions

## Purpose

This is Michael Wahba's professional portfolio. Its primary job is to help him get hired — as a generalist software engineer (any industry), a technical design lead, or an educator/instructor. Every content and copy decision should serve that goal.

## Target reader

A hiring manager or senior engineer who is scanning quickly. They may not know games industry jargon (Blueprints, WPF, UE5). When using technical terms that aren't universally known, give just enough context so a non-specialist understands what it means and why it matters. Don't over-explain, just don't assume.

## Tone

Warm and human. Someone you'd want on your team. Not a brag sheet, not a dry résumé. Write like a person, not a job posting. Personality is allowed.

## Writing style

- No em-dashes. Use commas, periods, or restructure the sentence instead.
- Keep copy natural and conversational. No stiff or overly formal phrasing.
- Never invent details (project descriptions, dates, roles, awards, press). If something is unclear or missing, ask before writing it.
- Lead with outcomes, not responsibilities. "Built X that does Y" beats "Responsible for X."
- No hedging verbs: not "helped with," "contributed to," or "assisted in." Mike led or built things. Say that.
- Avoid hollow adjectives (innovative, passionate, driven, cutting-edge). Let the work carry the weight.
- Short paragraphs. Hiring managers scan.

## Content priorities

- The four flagship projects (Maxis, Egregore, The Immersive Archive, Atrio) are the core of the site. Don't dilute or bury them.
- Mike's background is genuinely cross-disciplinary: CS + MFA + commercial shipping + teaching. That breadth is an asset for generalist and technical design roles. Lean into it rather than flattening it.
- When writing about teaching (Circuit Stream), frame it in terms of what it demonstrates: ability to communicate complex systems, experience leading learners, credibility in the field.
- Awards and press (AWE Best in Show, The Logic, CBC, IEEE) are strong signals to non-games readers. Make sure they stay visible and their significance is clear to an outsider.
